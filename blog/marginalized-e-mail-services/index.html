Protocol Imperialism: The OAuth2 Trap.

A didactic analysis of how proprietary authentication standards are weaponized to marginalize independent communication infrastructure. Exploring the technical delta between security and sovereignty.

Email was designed as a federated, decentralized protocol. However, the rise of "Managed Identity" (OAuth2) has created a new form of protocol imperialism. From a didactic perspective, OAuth2 is presented as a security upgrade, but in practice, it often acts as a gatekeeper that favors Big Tech incumbents at the expense of sovereign servers.

The Artificial Security Delta

Corporations often block "Less Secure Apps" (those using standard IMAP/SMTP) to force users into OAuth2-compliant clients. While the technical rationale is to prevent credential theft, the implementation is often didacticly biased: it whitelists corporate domains while flagging independent servers as "untrusted." This creates a "PC Magazine" level of frustration for users who value autonomy.

Building the Sovereign Response

The General Bots approach to communication sovereignty involves building our own high-fidelity connectors. Instead of relying on proprietary whitelists, we leverage: 1. **Sovereign OAuth2 Providers:** Self-hosted identity layers that follow open standards without corporate gatekeeping. 2. **BASIC Scripting for IMAP:** Deterministic automation of email workflows that bypasses the need for "Managed" client logic. 3. **Multimodal Orchestration:** Integrating email into the broader AI workflow without data exfiltration.

In the GLM 5.1 era, true value is found in systems that are *Independent by Design*. By acknowledging the OAuth2 trap, General Bots ensures that its partners and investors are building on a foundation of genuine sovereignty.
